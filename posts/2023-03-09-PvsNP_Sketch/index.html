Sketch of CAF’s proof that \(P eq NP\).

We sketch a measure-topological argument that \(P eq NP\). The key idea is that random search over an unstructured subset is an irreducible problem, here interpreted as an invariance of domain argument a la Brouwer. The formal development of these ideas will be subject of future post.

Here is a sketch of how to prove that \(P\) is not equal to \(NP\). The ideas are all based on discussions with C.A. Feinstein.

Measures and Algorithms:

  1. (Deciding supports is the universal decision problem) Every decision problem is equivalent to the problem of deciding the support of a measure \(\mu\). Or almost equivalently, of deciding the support of the image of a function \(F\). E.g. for the subset sum problem, the image is the pushforward onto \(\bf{Z}\) of the sum total \(\epsilon: 2^X \to {\bf{Z}}\).

  2. (Algorithms are compositions of measures, equivalently paths in the category of finite measure spaces) An algorithm to solve a decision problem is a composition of the measure \(\mu\) or the function \(F\) into intermediate measures or function evaluations. Here we comment that there is a natural composition of measures, c.f. the proofs of triangle inequalities in Wasserstein spaces.

#What is the Complexity of a Measure or Function or Algorithm?

The classic definition of computational complexity counts the number of time steps required, or number of calls to RAM or function evaluations, in the worst-possible case. We require here a definition of complexity for measures. The definition must be absolutely simple, and we can only say that the complexity of a measure is the “size” of its support. In this sense, complexity is equivalent to “entropy” as defined by Gromov [insert ref].

  1. (Complexity and Entropy: the Same Quantity) The complexity of a measure or a function is the “size” of the support. Complexity is subadditive (or superadditive?) with respect to composition. This means that decomposing \(F\) into a very long composition \(F=\cdots \circ F_i \circ F_{i-1} \circ \cdots\) does not necessarily reduce the complexity.

Every Algorithm Is a Composition of Random Searches

  1. (Algorithmic Factorization Theorem) Every algorithm is a composition of deterministic function evaluations and random search over a subset. (This requires more formal definition and proof). This also formalizes the idea that Lebesgue measure on a set amounts to random search on the support.

Random Search is Computationally Irreducible

  1. (Invariance of Domain and Irreducibility of Measure Complexity) Brouwer-Lebesgue Invariance of Domain theorem applies. This says there do not exist continuous injective measure-preserving maps from \(R^n\) to smaller dimension \(R^k\) for \(k < n\).

The items 1., 2., …, 5. if they can be rigorously established will essentially prove that \(P \neq NP\).
